## Practical Advice for Medical School Obsidian Vaults

Setting up Obsidian for medical school requires a strategic approach to prevent your vault from becoming an unmanageable mess. 

**Avoid Over-Linking:** It is tempting to turn every medical term into a bidirectional link. However, linking every instance of "hypertension" across 5,000 notes creates a useless, noisy graph. Reserve links for broad concepts, high-yield differential diagnoses, or specific drug classes.

**Standardize Your Metadata:** Decide on a tag and folder structure early. Use YAML frontmatter consistently. For example, every disease note should have metadata for `system` (e.g., cardiovascular), `type` (e.g., pathology), and `high_yield_status`. This discipline is what makes plugins like Dataview functional.

**Embrace the Zettelkasten Cautiously:** While atomic notes (one idea per note) work well for philosophy, medicine often requires rapid synthesis. A single, comprehensive note on "Asthma" containing pathophysiology, presentation, and treatment is usually more practical for board review than 15 heavily linked micro-notes. Use heading links (`[[Asthma#Treatment]]`) to reference specific sections instead of fracturing the topic.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need to know how to code to use Obsidian in medical school?
No, coding is not required. While plugins like Dataview and Templater offer advanced features utilizing JavaScript, their basic functionalities—which are more than sufficient for most medical students—can be learned in a few hours using simple, well-documented syntax found in community tutorials.

### How does Obsidian compare to Notion for medical students?
Obsidian is local, offline, and significantly faster than Notion, which is critical when you have spotty hospital Wi-Fi. Furthermore, Obsidian's seamless integration with Anki via plugins is vastly superior to Notion's current capabilities, making it the better choice for intensive memorization.

### Can Obsidian handle large PDF textbooks?
Yes, but with caveats. Obsidian can store and link to large PDFs, and plugins like Annotator can read them. However, for massive 2,000-page textbooks, dedicated PDF readers are often smoother. The best workflow is usually reading in a dedicated app and exporting summaries or deep links into Obsidian.

### Is it safe to put patient information in Obsidian?
Obsidian stores files locally on your hard drive, which is more secure than cloud-based apps. However, to comply with HIPAA and hospital policies, you must NEVER include Protected Health Information (PHI) like patient names, dates of birth, or medical record numbers in your vault. Always aggressively anonymize any clinical logs.

### Should I sync my Obsidian vault across devices?
Yes, accessing your notes on a smartphone or tablet during ward rounds is invaluable. You can use Obsidian Sync (paid, end-to-end encrypted) or free alternatives like iCloud Drive or syncthing, though you must ensure your chosen method complies with your institution's data security guidelines regarding anonymized clinical notes.
